What are these metrics?

If you're not a scientist, don't worry! Here is a simple guide to what each metric means:

PM2.5 (Fine Dust): Tiny particles from car exhaust or smoke that can enter your bloodstream.
PM10 (Coarse Dust): Larger dust from construction or dirt roads that irritates the lungs.
NO2 (Nitrogen Dioxide): Toxic gas from burning fuel, causes asthma and coughing.
CO (Carbon Monoxide): Poisonous gas from vehicles and machinery.
SO2 (Sulfur Dioxide): Harsh fumes mostly from industrial factories.
O3 (Ozone): "Bad" ozone created when sunlight reacts with smog at ground level.

Health Cautionary & Advisory

Understand how different levels of AQI impact your health and what actions to take.

0 - 50: GOOD

Air quality is considered satisfactory, and air pollution poses little or no risk.

51 - 100: MODERATE

Air quality is acceptable. Unusually sensitive people should consider limiting prolonged outdoor exertion.

101 - 150: UNHEALTHY FOR SENSITIVE PEOPLE

Sensitive people may experience health effects. The general public is less likely to be affected.

151 - 200: UNHEALTHY

Everyone may begin to experience health effects. Wear masks and avoid prolonged outdoor activity.

> 300: HAZARDOUS

Health warning of emergency conditions. The entire population is more likely to be affected. Stay indoors, run air purifiers.

Pollutant Glossary: What Are We Measuring?

Pollutant Definition & Primary Sources Health Impact
PM2.5 Fine Particulate Matter (<2.5 µm) from vehicle exhaust and power plants. Can penetrate deep into the lungs and blood, causing severe respiratory/cardiovascular issues.
PM10 Coarse Particulate Matter from construction sites, unpaved roads, and crushing operations. Inhalable dust that irritates airways and frequently triggers severe asthma attacks.
NO2 Nitrogen Dioxide, a highly reactive gas emitted by heavy vehicle traffic and fossil fuels. Highly suffocating; causes severe lung inflammation and reduces immunity to infections.
SO2 Sulfur Dioxide, a toxic gas produced by burning sulfur-heavy fossil fuels like coal. Causes chest tightness, breathing pain, and contributes heavily to acid rain formation.
CO Carbon Monoxide, an odorless gas resulting from incomplete fuel burning in traffic. Deprives the body of vital oxygen, causing rapid onset of headaches and dizziness.
O3 Ground-Level Ozone, a reactive gas created when sunlight bakes other urban pollutants. Irritates the throat, causes chest pain, and forms the main chemical ingredient of smog.
